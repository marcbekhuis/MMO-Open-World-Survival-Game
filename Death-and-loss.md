# Death and Loss

Death in this world is a setback with teeth, not a slap on the wrist and not the end of a character. Every trip beyond a safe city's walls is a wager, and what a player stands to lose turns above all on whether the wilderness or another player is the one who brings them down. This document sets out what happens at the moment of death, what is at risk, and how a player gets back on their feet, and it is the rule set referred to wherever the rest of the design speaks of what a defeated player loses.

## The Corpse and What Is at Risk

When a character falls, they leave a lootable corpse holding what they were carrying: the contents of the bag, pouches, and hand slots described in [Inventory](Inventory.md), down to and including equipped gear. Carrying capacity is therefore also exposure, and the weight a player hauls into danger is precisely the weight they can lose. The one reliable exception is the mage's [Item Box](Magic/Spells/Misc/Item-box.md), whose stored contents sit outside the body entirely and never appear on the corpse, which is part of what makes the spell so prized. Goods banked in a settlement or a player's own base are never at risk from a death in the field; only what is carried is.

What death never takes is who the character has become. The levels, stats, and use-earned skills set out in [Player Progression](Player.md) are untouched, because progression here is the record of what a player has done and is not something the design strips away on a bad night. There is no permadeath.

## Dying to the World

A death to a creature, a fall, the cold, or any other hazard of the wilderness is meant to sting and then be survivable. The corpse persists for a recovery window, and a player can make the run back to reclaim what they left, so the true cost of a lonely death in the wilds is the dangerous return trip, the wear it puts on equipment, and the handful of perishables and loose resources the world claims in the meantime. This is the moderate, recoverable outcome that lets the wilderness punish carelessness without erasing an afternoon's work, and it is why a careful player treats a corpse run as the second half of a bad fight rather than a lost cause.

The catch is that the world is not the only thing that may find the body. In contested country a corpse left on the ground is an opportunity for any player who happens past, so even a death to a wild beast can become a total loss if someone reaches it first. Distance from safety raises the stakes of every death, by design.

## Dying to Another Player

When the killer is another player, the gloves come off: the victor may strip the corpse completely. This is full loot, everything carried and everything worn, and it is the central stake of the open world. It is the reason the frontier's first rule is never to carry what you are not prepared to lose, the reason a laden merchant or a richly geared raider is a target worth hunting, and the engine that keeps gear genuinely valuable, since anything that can be won can also be lost. What changes hands this way does not vanish from the world but recirculates through it, feeding the player-run markets of [Economy and Trade](Economy.md).

Full loot is deliberately harsh, and the design does not pretend otherwise. Its counterweights are the safe cities and reputation consequences set out in [Conflict and PvP](Conflict-and-pvp.md), the player's own freedom to decide how much to risk on any given outing, and the storage systems that let wealth be banked rather than worn into a fight.

## Getting Back on Your Feet

A fallen player respawns at a bound safe location, by default the capital or spawn city they are tied to, or a bed or claimed point established through the [Building System](Building-system.md) so that groups working the frontier can return closer to the action. From there the choice is immediate and familiar to the genre: re-equip from storage and cut the losses, or gear up and race back to the corpse before the window closes or a stranger arrives. Either way the loop turns again, a little wiser about what to bring next time.
