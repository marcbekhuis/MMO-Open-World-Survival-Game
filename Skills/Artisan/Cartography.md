# Cartography

In most games a map is universal, detailed and updated for every player the moment they step into a place. This world does it differently: cartography is a skill, and a usable map is something a player makes rather than something they are simply given. That single change turns exploration and map-making into a specialised, valuable pursuit, and gives the patient cartographer a real place in the world's economy and its social life.

## How Maps Grow

Every player starts with only a rudimentary map, showing coastlines, major rivers, and the rough shape of the terrain and little else. Detail is earned by levelling the cartography skill: at the lowest ranks a map carries only basic outlines of water, settlements, and roads, and as the skill climbs it fills in progressively, adding terrain coloured by biome, points of interest like caves, ruins, and dungeon entrances, the locations of crafting resources, and the names of cities, regions, and landmarks. At its highest rank, cartography produces fully detailed, full-colour maps that hold everything a traveller could want, from named locations and dungeon mouths to resource nodes and the fine grain of the terrain itself.

## Levelling the Skill

The skill advances above all through discovery. A player earns experience each time they chart new ground, which keeps cartography bound to genuine exploration rather than grinding in place. Revisiting already-charted areas after a level-up can tease out further detail and a little more experience, but only a little, not enough to carry the skill on its own. Real progress comes from crafting map items, the act of committing explored ground to a shareable record, so a cartographer levels by going out, seeing the world, and setting it down.

## Map Items and the Player Economy

A crafted map item captures the detail of a specific area, its landmarks, dungeon entrances, and resource nodes, in a form that can be handed to another player. This makes maps a commodity, and a valuable one, especially to players who live or die by knowing where to find resources, where danger lies, and how to reach hidden places. Cartographers sell and trade their work into the markets of [Economy and Trade](../../Economy.md), and players who never invest a point in the skill themselves can still buy their way to good maps, which knits cartography into the wider web of cooperation and exchange between players.

## The Cartographer's Role

Cartography offers a rare blend of utility and prestige. A skilled mapmaker is not only a producer of goods but a source of knowledge, and the best of them become guides as much as merchants, trading in routes and warnings as readily as in maps, and earning real reputation in the player community for the accuracy and reach of their work. The pull of the skill is easy to see in practice: an adventurer hunting a dungeon buried in dense forest could search blindly and risk a great deal, or buy a master cartographer's map marking the entrance, the nearby hazards, and the resources along the way, and turn a dangerous guess into a planned expedition. In a world this large and this lethal, the people who document it hold a quiet kind of power.
