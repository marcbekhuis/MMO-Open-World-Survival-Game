# Skills Overview

Skills define active and passive abilities learned through use, training, and investment. They sit beside character stats from [Player Progression](Player.md): stats describe a character's broad physical and mental strengths, while skills describe what the character has practised enough to do well.

## Primary Skill Families

- [Combat](Skills/Combat.md) - Weapon handling, defensive maneuvers, battlefield movement, and role-specific techniques.
- **Magic** - Spellcasting disciplines and elemental mastery. See [Magic](Magic.md) for spell progression and upgrade rules.
- **Gathering** - Resource-focused skills such as logging, mining, harvesting, skinning, fishing, and salvage.
- **Artisan** - Crafting, refinement, construction support, repair, and specialist production. See [Cartography](Skills/Artisan/Cartography.md) for an example artisan-adjacent utility skill.
- **Survival** - Navigation, campcraft, weather resistance, cooking, tracking, and wilderness preparation.
- **Taming and handling** - Long-term interaction with trainable animals, mounts, pack creatures, or utility companions where the design supports them.
- **Social and trade** - Bartering, reputation benefits, settlement services, and guild-facing utility that improves non-combat play.

## Progression Model

Skills improve through repeated use, dedicated training resources, and occasional milestone choices. A player should feel steady progress from ordinary play, but deeper specialization should require intention. Broadly, early ranks unlock reliability, middle ranks unlock new actions or recipes, and high ranks unlock specializations that change how the player approaches a role.

Unlock cadence should be readable rather than rigid. A skill can offer small benefits frequently, such as improved yield or reduced stamina cost, while saving major branch choices for meaningful thresholds. This lets players experiment early before committing to a long-term identity.

## Design Notes

No single skill family should be mandatory for all players. Combat specialists need ways to survive and contribute economically, crafters need routes into dangerous materials through trade or group play, and explorers need tools that make information valuable. The best version of the skill system creates interdependence without making solo play feel empty.
