# Non-Player Characters

Non-player characters are the human, elven, and dwarven people who make settlements feel inhabited rather than merely serviced. They are merchants, guards, craftspeople, trainers, travellers, farmers, performers, mages, and civilians, all tied into the routines, trade, danger, and reputation systems that make the world feel lived in.

## Roles and Routines

NPCs are readable by role at a glance while still feeling like people who belong to a place. Merchants move goods between cities and frontier settlements, guards hold gates and patrol roads, city mages maintain protective work around crowded hubs, and craftspeople visibly perform the labour that keeps a settlement functioning. Civilians such as farmers, bartenders, musicians, weavers, butchers, and builders add ordinary life around the services players use. Children appear only in safe, non-combat routines where they add life to urban spaces without becoming targets or rewards.

These roles scale with settlement size. A roadside outpost has a handful of armed guards, a trader, and a tired cook; a capital city supports patrols, guild representatives, trainers, specialist vendors, craftsmen, performers, and city mages. The larger the settlement, the more its NPCs communicate its power, wealth, and vulnerabilities through what they are seen doing.

## Interaction and Services

NPCs provide the stable layer of play that lets the world support many lives within it. They offer quests, escort requests, protection work, delivery jobs, vendors, trainers, crafting stations, repair, rumours, and settlement services. The [Quest System](Quests.md) uses them as the face of local needs, while [Economy and Trade](Economy.md) uses merchants and craftsmen to anchor prices, stock basic goods, and give non-crafters a complete way to stay supplied.

Some NPC routines connect directly to wider systems. Travelling merchants create convoy routes that can be escorted, ambushed, taxed, or avoided. Guards and mages define the practical edge of a safe city, shaping how players behave around settlements and how the [Reputation System](Reputation-system.md) responds to theft, murder, defence, and public service. City mages use [Nature Growth](Magic/Spells/Earth/Nature-growth.md) around crowded starting areas to restore over-harvested local resources, with cooldowns and limits so the spell supports new-player density without becoming an exploitable production loop.

## World Events

Because NPCs move through the same world as players, they generate trouble as well as services. A merchant convoy delayed on the road, a guard patrol that fails to return, a village musician carrying rumours between inns, or a mage sent to repair depleted groves can all become the beginning of an event rather than a static menu. These events tie settlements to the wilderness around them: players meet civilisation on the road, not only behind walls.

NPCs share the player races' visual language so they feel like part of the same societies described in [Races](Races.md), with variety coming from clothing, armour, accessories, posture, animation, and the work they are performing. This makes each settlement legible as a place where people live, need things, fear things, and remember what players do.
