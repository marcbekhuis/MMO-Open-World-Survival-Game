# Guilds and Factions

Organisations are how the social world holds its shape between individual players and the world at large. Two kinds matter, and the design keeps them distinct. World factions are the NPC-run powers a player acts with or against, the realms, settlements, and orders whose standing is tracked by the [Reputation System](Reputation-system.md). Player guilds are the organisations players build for themselves, from a handful of friends to a territory-holding war machine. Both are referenced throughout [Conflict and PvP](Conflict-and-pvp.md) and [Raids and Conquest](Raids.md); this document is their home, and the place that explains how they relate.

## World Factions

The world is already populated by powers with their own interests. Foremost are the three expanding realms of [Setting and Lore](Setting-and-lore.md), the human, elven, and dwarven peoples whose race-cultures are described in [Races](Races.md), each pushing into the reopened frontier on its own terms and as ready to contest one another as to share the spoils. Beneath and beside them sit individual settlements, sworn orders, trade combines, and the wild's own powers, the goblin war-hosts and monstrous dominions that hold ground against every realm. A player's relationship with each of these is the standing tracked by the reputation system, which is why the same act can make a hero in one faction's eyes and an enemy in another's. Factions give the world its politics: they post the bounties, hire the escorts, raise the patrols, and offer the quests that turn a player's choices into consequences.

## Player Guilds

A guild is a player organisation with a name, a membership, and shared purpose. At the smallest scale it is a band of friends pooling effort and storage; at the largest it is a standing power that fields armies, holds territory, and shapes the politics of a region. A guild's anchor is its hall, raised through the [Building System](Building-system.md) and detailed among the [Structures](Structures.md), which serves as home, muster point, shared storage, and the visible seat of the group's identity and ambition. Guilds give the cooperative end of the game its structure, turning the loose grouping of individual play into something that can plan, hold, and be remembered, while the design keeps solo and small-group play fully viable so that joining one is a choice rather than a requirement.

## Territory and Conquest

The prize that organised play contests is ground. A guild that holds a stretch of frontier holds what is on it, the resource nodes, the trade routes of [Economy and Trade](Economy.md), and the defensible sites worth building on, and holding it means defending it through the organised warfare of [Raids and Conquest](Raids.md). Territory is never wholly secure: a held region draws rivals, and a guild's reach is only as long as its ability to answer a raid, so power on the frontier is something continuously defended rather than permanently owned. This churn of claim, defence, and loss is the engine the setting describes, a frontier opening faster than anyone can hold it, and player guilds are among its principal players.

## Alliances and Enmity

Between guilds and with the world's factions, diplomacy is its own layer of play. Guilds strike alliances to share defence and trade, negotiate access and tribute, and turn on one another when the balance shifts, while their standing with the NPC factions opens or closes whole regions to them. The reputation system's support for mixed standing, hero here and villain there, is what makes this possible without flattening every relationship into a single score, so smuggling, mercenary work, double-dealing, and hard-won trust all have room to operate. The strongest position on the frontier is rarely the one with the highest walls; more often it is the one with the right friends and the fewest enemies able to act at once.
