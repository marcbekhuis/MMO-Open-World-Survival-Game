# Taming and Control
Unlike other games where taming a creature provides permanent control, this game introduces a dynamic taming system that requires players to actively maintain control using one of three methods. The stronger your control over a creature, the better it will respond to commands, particularly when being ridden.
## Slave Bonding
Slave bonding is the easiest yet most dangerous method of controlling a creature. This involves using harmful and coercive methods to force the creature to obey your commands. However, failing to maintain control can have serious consequences: the creature may either flee or retaliate, potentially attacking the player with the intent to kill, depending on its nature.
## Parental Bonding
Parental bonding offers the strongest control over a creature but is also the most challenging method. To achieve this, players must raise a creature from birth or near birth. This can be done by breeding creatures under the player’s control (requiring creatures of both genders) or by obtaining wild eggs or baby creatures (for mammals). While creatures raised this way build a deep bond with the player, maintaining their trust is crucial. If neglected, they can revert to their wild instincts and break free from the player's control.
## Trust Bonding
Trust bonding is the slowest method but allows players to tame wild creatures without resorting to harmful tactics. This process requires players to earn the creature’s trust over time through consistent care, such as proper feeding and avoiding overly dangerous situations. Even after trust is established, players must continue nurturing the bond to keep the creature under their control.

## Creature Riding

Riding a creature here is not the same as driving a vehicle. In most games a mount becomes an extension of the player, moving exactly as commanded, but this game takes a deliberately different approach in which a rider guides the creature's own AI rather than puppeting its every step. A mount keeps its autonomy and its instincts, and above all its will to survive: it will refuse commands that would clearly destroy it, balking at a leap off a cliff, a headlong charge into something far stronger, or a plunge into water deep enough to drown in. The animal is a partner with a sense of self-preservation, not a set of controls.

This is why the bond built through the methods above matters at every moment in the saddle, not only at the point of taming. The stronger a rider's control, the more readily a creature will follow guidance into danger it would otherwise refuse, while a weak or fraying bond leaves the creature's instincts in charge, so that it second-guesses, hesitates, or simply declines. A rider therefore learns to work with the animal's behaviour rather than override it, shaping their plans around what it will and will not do. The result is a relationship that feels dynamic and authentic, where reaching a goal on creatureback is a matter of cooperation between rider and mount rather than the mount doing exactly as it is told.
