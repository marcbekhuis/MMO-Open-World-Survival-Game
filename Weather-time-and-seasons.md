# Weather, Time, and Seasons

A living world needs a living sky. Time, weather, and the turning of the seasons are what keep the seamless world of [World Design](World.md) from feeling static: they change how a place looks, what it costs to cross, what hunts in it, and when it is wise to be there at all. Because the world is a single persistent space shared by everyone in it, as set out in [Server Architecture](Server-architecture.md), these systems run world-wide rather than per-player, so that nightfall, a gathering storm, or the first hard frost of winter is something players experience together and plan around as a community.

## Day and Night

A shared world clock carries the world through dawn, day, dusk, and night, the same hour for every player standing in the same place. Daylight is the safe, legible time, when landmarks are visible and routes are easy to read; night narrows the world, rewards light sources and the survival skills of [Skills](Skills.md), and brings out creatures that keep to the dark. The cycle is a tactical variable as much as an atmospheric one, since a crossing that is trivial at noon can be treacherous after dark, and players learn to time their travel, their hunting, and their raids to the hour. The one place the world clock does not reach is inside a dungeon, where the impossible interiors of [Dungeons](Structures/Dungeons.md) keep their own time and a sealed floor can hold midnight while the sun is high outside.

## Weather

Weather is the world's short-term mood and its most immediate hazard. Rain and fog cut visibility and muffle or carry sound, storms make footing treacherous and can ground or endanger anything in the open, and the biome-specific extremes, the sandstorms of the [Scorching Desert](Biomes/Scorching-desert.md) and the blizzards of the [Tundra](Biomes/Tundra.md), can turn a routine journey into a fight for survival on their own. Weather reaches directly into combat and travel: a downpour that kills a campfire, a fog that hides an ambush, or a gale that scatters arrows changes how a fight or a crossing plays out. The fiercest weather even stirs the world's deeper forces, and severe storms at sea are exactly the conditions that draw the [Kraken](Creatures/Kraken.md) up from the deep, so a darkening sky over open water is a warning in more ways than one.

## Seasons

Over a longer cycle the seasons reshape the world wholesale. Summer opens the high passes and lengthens the working day; autumn is the season of harvest and provisioning; winter shortens the days, deepens the cold that [Survival](Survival.md) turns against an unprepared traveller, freezes routes that ran open in summer, and makes food and shelter genuinely scarce; spring thaws the world and renews it. Seasons change what the land offers as much as what it costs, so the resources a gatherer relies on, the game a hunter tracks, and the windows in which certain regions are passable all shift with the calendar. A frontier that is generous in late summer can be lethal by midwinter, which gives the world's communities a shared rhythm of preparation and risk.

## How It Reaches the Player

Time and weather are not backdrop; they are inputs the rest of the design reads. They drive the exposure and food pressures of [Survival](Survival.md), shape the routes and timing of [Travel and Mounts](Travel-and-mounts.md), and change the readability of a fight without ever altering the underlying rules, in keeping with the principle that what one player can see every player can see. Because the cycle is shared and persistent, it becomes something players coordinate around: mounting an expedition in a clear season, timing a raid to fall under cover of night or storm, or racing to lay in supplies before the first snow closes a pass.
