# New Player Experience

A harsh, full-loot world has to be careful about how it greets a newcomer, because the same stakes that make the frontier thrilling can drive away a player who meets them in the first ten minutes with no idea what happened. The answer here is a guided, protected start that climbs steeply rather than a softened core: the early hours teach the game in safety, and the real danger arrives only when the player chooses to walk toward it. This document covers how a character is made and how a player is brought into the world.

## Character Creation

A new player begins by making a character from one of the three races set out in [Player Progression](Player.md), with deep control over appearance, the face, build, hair, colouring, and the scars and wear that make a character recognisably their own, rendered at the fidelity described in [Art Direction](Art-direction.md). What they do not choose at creation is a class, because there are none. Identity is not picked from a menu but earned in play: the stats invested and the skills practised over a character's life are what turn them into a duelist, a siege-breaker, a cartographer, or a smith, so two characters of the same race can grow into entirely different roles. Creation sets the starting point and the face in the mirror; the world writes the rest.

## The First Hours

A new character wakes in or beside one of the capital cities, inside the ring of safety that the NPC guards of [Conflict and PvP](Conflict-and-pvp.md) hold against violence. Here the game teaches itself in the open rather than from behind a wall of pop-ups: gathering, crafting, combat, inventory, and survival are introduced through early tasks and the ordinary business of a living city, supported by the [Quest System](Quests.md) and the readable, on-demand interface of [UI and HUD](UI-and-HUD.md). Starter gear is cheap and easily replaced, so the first lessons in fighting and dying cost little, and the patrolled land around the capital gives a newcomer room to practise, explore, and fail without losing everything to another player while they are still finding their feet.

## Crossing into the Frontier

The safety is real, but it has an edge, and crossing it is the player's own decision. Travel outward and the patrols thin, the guarantees fall away, and the full-loot frontier of [Death and Loss](Death-and-loss.md) opens up, exactly as steeply as the design intends. Nothing forces a player across that line before they are ready, and everything rewards them for choosing it when they are. This is the sharp climb: the game never pretends the frontier is gentle, it simply makes sure a player knows the rules, and has something worth protecting, before the frontier gets to test them. The first law of the wilds, never carry what you cannot afford to lose, lands far harder on a player who has already learned, in safety, what it means to lose.
